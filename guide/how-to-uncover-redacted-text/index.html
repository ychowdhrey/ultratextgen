How to Uncover Redacted Text (and When You Can't)

Whether blacked-out text can be revealed comes down to one thing: was the original text deleted, or just covered up? Here's how to tell the difference — and how to redact so nobody can read what you hid.

Privacy ⏱ 5 min read Redaction, Explained

Key Takeaways

  • Unicode redaction is permanent. When letters are replaced with █ blocks, the originals are gone — there is nothing to uncover.
  • Image redaction is only as good as the method. A black box over a still-present text layer, or blur/pixelation, can sometimes be reversed.
  • "Revealed" leaks usually aren't decoded. They come from a sloppy redaction that never removed the underlying data, not from cracking the blackout itself.
  • To hide text safely, delete it — don't cover it. Replace the words before sharing, or use a real redaction tool that strips the underlying text.
The Short Answer

It Depends on Whether the Text Was Deleted or Just Covered

"How do I uncover redacted text?" is one of the most-searched questions about blacked-out content — and the honest answer is that most of the time you can't, and that's the point. Real redaction removes information. Bad redaction merely hides it behind a shape, leaving the original sitting underneath.

So before asking whether a blackout can be reversed, ask how it was made. There are two completely different kinds.

Case 1 — Text Redaction

Unicode Blackouts Can't Be Uncovered

When you run a sentence through a redacted text generator, each letter is replaced with a block character such as █, ▓, ■ or ⬛. The result is just text — the word agent literally becomes █████. The original letters are not stored anywhere in the output, so:

  • Highlighting or selecting it reveals nothing.
  • There is no "decoder" — there is no hidden layer to decode.
  • Copy it, paste it, and the original words are permanently gone.

This is exactly why Unicode redaction is great for joke spoilers, SCP-style entries, and privacy-friendly screenshots: once you copy the blacked-out version, even you can't get the original back from it.

Case 2 — Image & PDF Redaction

Boxes, Blur, and Pixelation Can Sometimes Be Reversed

The leaks you hear about — where someone "revealed" redacted text — almost never involve breaking a true blackout. They involve a redaction that never deleted the underlying data:

  • Black box over live text. In many PDFs a rectangle is drawn on top, but the selectable text is still there. Copy-pasting underneath, or removing the shape, exposes it.
  • Blur and pixelation. These transform the original rather than remove it. For predictable content, the transformation can sometimes be estimated or reversed.
  • Flattened, but low-resolution. Even a flattened image can leak short, guessable content (a name, a date) through context.

The takeaway isn't "you can always uncover it" — it's that covering is not the same as removing. If the original data survives anywhere in the file, the redaction is only as strong as someone's willingness to dig.

Do It Right

How to Black Out Text So It Stays Hidden

If your goal is to hide something for good:

  • For posts, chats, and screenshots: replace the words before you share them. A Unicode redacted text generator swaps the letters for blocks, so what you paste never contained the secret. Use the Selective style to black out only the words you choose.
  • For documents: use a real redaction feature that deletes the underlying text, then export a fresh, flattened copy and check that nothing is selectable beneath the marks.
  • Never rely on a black rectangle drawn over live text, a highlighter set to black, or a blur filter.

And the reverse rule, for anyone hoping to read someone else's redaction: if it was done properly, there is nothing to recover — by design.
