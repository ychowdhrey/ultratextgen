What is upside down text?

Upside down text is ordinary writing converted into flipped Unicode characters — each letter is swapped for its 180°-rotated twin (a → ɐ, e → ǝ, h → ɥ) and the order is reversed, so the whole phrase reads as if it were turned over. Because the output is standard Unicode and not an image, you can copy and paste it straight into a bio, caption, comment, message, or document and it renders the same everywhere.

The five styles, and when to use each

Most generators give you one flip. The single most common mistake is using the wrong mode for the job — a full reverse ruins a multi-line caption, and a per-line flip ruins the phone-rotation trick. Here is which to pick:

Upside Down (flip + reverse)

The true 180° rotation. Characters are flipped and the order reversed, so the text reads correctly when the page or phone is turned over. This is the style for the "I love you, turn your phone upside down" trick.

Backwards (reverse only)

Reverses the character order without flipping — a backwards / mirror-writing effect, useful for puzzles and reversed reading.

Mirror

A left-to-right mirrored illusion using look-alike characters — a different effect from a true vertical flip.

Flip Each Line

Flips every line in place but keeps your lines in order, so multi-line bios and captions stay readable top-to-bottom instead of having the last line jump to the top.

Last Word

Flips only the final word of each line — or anything you wrap in [[double brackets]] — to emphasise one word while the rest stays upright.

Upside down cross & dark symbols

Searching for an inverted cross? Use the ⸸ Cross wrap above to bracket your text with the inverted-cross glyph. One thing worth knowing: the symbol carries three different meanings depending on context.

Cross of St. Peter (Christian): a symbol of humility — Peter asked to be crucified upside down, feeling unworthy to die as Christ did.

Anti-Christian / satanic (pop culture): popularised by horror films and metal imagery; this is the meaning many people assume.

Aesthetic (y2k / emo / dark romance): purely decorative for usernames and bios, with no religious intent.

Upside down question mark: typing ¿ vs. the SMS bug

There are two completely different reasons people look this up — make sure you're in the right one:

You want to type ¿ or ¡ (Spanish)

These are the inverted opening question and exclamation marks Spanish sentences start with. Type them above (? → ¿, ! → ¡ in the flip), or on a phone long-press the ?/! key.

You're getting ¿ you never asked for

If your text messages arrive full of upside-down question marks where spaces or line breaks should be, that's an SMS encoding bug, not flipped text. SMS has no newline character, so multiple spaces or repeated returns get encoded as a control byte the receiving phone draws as ¿ — most often on iPhone→Android and certain carriers. Avoid double spaces and extra line breaks, or drop an emoji in to reset the encoding.
